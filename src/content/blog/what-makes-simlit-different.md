SimLit sits in a strange and interesting position in the fan creativity landscape. It is not quite fanfiction as most people understand it, and not quite a game diary either.

In most fanfiction, you are writing with pre-existing characters — characters with established voices, histories, arcs. The reader comes in with expectations, and part of the craft is honouring those expectations while doing something interesting with them. SimLit is different. Your characters begin as shapes you made from sliders. They have no voice until you give them one. They have no history until you decide what happened before the story started. The game hands you a person-shaped space and you fill it.

This is, I think, both the freedom and the difficulty of the form.

The Sims gives you architecture, gesture, light. It gives you a particular kind of visual language — that warm, slightly oversaturated palette, those small domestic moments — that becomes part of the storytelling even when you are writing in prose rather than captions. When I describe a room in [Tales of Berrinmoore](/story/tales-of-berrinmoore), I am drawing on thousands of hours of looking at rooms through a camera I control entirely. There is a way the afternoon light comes through a bay window in-game that I have never seen anywhere else, and it has shaped how I write light.

The other thing SimLit does, and this is less talked about, is that it forces a particular kind of intimacy. You are not writing grand adventures. You are writing the moment someone stands at the kitchen counter and doesn't know what to think. You are writing the way two people don't look at each other at a party. The Sims is, at its heart, a game about the small textures of daily life — bills, meals, moods, the slow accumulation of a relationship's history — and SimLit inherits that. Even the most dramatic SimLit tends to be dramatic in a domestic key.

That is the part I love most about it. The smallness that turns out not to be small at all.
